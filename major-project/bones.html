Bones

History

Bones was founded by ex-Sunrise staff members, making it a grandchild of Mushi Production much like MAPPA is. Sunrise is primarily known for its contributions to the mecha genre- it created the massive Mobile Suit Gundam franchise, Patlabor, and Code Geass. Largely, Sunrise's shows existed to sell toys, like their cult-classic Cowboy Bebop, which is one of the most well-known anime in the US. Sunrise formed the new studio to animate the Cowboy Bebop spinoff movie. Bones produced many acclaimed anime from the 2000's, such as Fullmetal Alchemist, Ouran High School Host Club, Eureka Seven, and Soul Eater. In 2007, one of the cofounders of Bones sadly passed away.

Specialty

One of Bones' most interesting qualities that they inherited from Sunrise is that they are not just one studio, but 6! Studios A-F All have different styles, strengths, and work on different projects. Studio A is known for keeping tight schedules and outsourcing for other studios. Studio B has the highest quality animation, particularly, their work on Mob Psycho 100 is of standout excellence. The animation in that show is highly experimental and sets the show apart from its sister series by Madhouse, One Punch Man. Studio C was created a few years after the founding to handle the production of Fullmetal Alchemist. Likewise, Studio D was formed to handle Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood. Studios E and F were formed very recently and have little under their belt as of now.

A Tale of Two Alchemists

In the early 2000's, Fullmetal Alchemist was a smash hit manga and ripe for an anime adaptation, which Bones was given the rights to make. This was in an era of great transition, where cel animation was being phased out and digital animation was becoming the norm. Fullmetal Alchemist would be the last fully cel animated show that Bones would ever make. The story centers around two alchemist brothers who had their bodies stolen from them as punishment for committing the taboo of human transmutation. Now armed with automail prosthetics for their troubles, the two set out to join the military and research how they can get their bodies back. Ripe with fashionable characters and in-depth worldbuilding, FMA seemed like it was going to be a bombastic send-off to cel animated history. There was just one problem: author Hiromu Arakawa explicitly did not want the anime to have the same manga, so not to spoil her readers. Bones would have to figure out their own story for the Elric brothers to follow, with minimal guidance from Arakawa. The result was what is now known as Fullmetal Alchemist '03, a gritty reimagining of the story that takes a cross-dimensional turn into mid-20th century Europe. Then, years after the original manga had been completed, Bones aimed for a more faithful adaptation that fans had been begging for. This was Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood. The only problem was, FMA:B was now a fully digital production, and completely lacked the slick edge of the original. Fans are pretty torn on which they like better: The original had much more emotional character development and darker moments, but the storyline of FMA:B is so narratively tight- dozens of characters have balanced arcs and satisfying conclusions. It's also a more thematically sound story as a whole whereas '03 has a number of plot holes. For a long time, FMA:B held the top spot on MyAnimeList, te premiere anime review website, before it lost the spot to Frieren: Beyond Journey's End. However, numbers aren't everything, and many argue that there's something special about '03 that Brotherhood simply fails to capture. You should take a peek at both and decide for yourself which one is superior.
