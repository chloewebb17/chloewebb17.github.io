Gainax

History

Gainax was originally formed under the name "Daicon Film" by students from Osaka University of Arts including the legendary Hideaki Anno, who would go on to create his and the company's magnum opus. But before we get there, Daicon Film was hard at work making short films for science-fiction conventions, causing its members to neglect their schoolwork and drop out of college due to the enormous amount of effort they were putting in. After getting expelled from Osaka University of Arts, Hideaki Anno independantly went to work on the film Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind, which was the first film by the most celebrated anime director of all time, two-time Oscar winner Hayao Miyazaki. Those who worked on this film, besides Anno, went on to form Studio Ghibli, who I will not be talking about on this site because they are their own behemoth of a studio. After returning to Daicon Film, Anno and the others decided to rename themselves Gainax and begin working on larger projects.

Specialty

Gainax always produced original projects, unlike most anime studios that adapt manga properties for TV. Some of Gainax's original projects became the most celebrated, influential anime of all time, such as Neon Genesis Evangelion. Gainax originals are known for their controversial twist endings, and Evangelion may be one of the best examples of that (though I won't spoil it here!). Words can't really do Evangelion's impact justice, so just know that all anime can basically be divided into two eras: pre-and-post Eva. Some other interesting projects include FLCL, a bold, eclectic 6-episode miniseries that was massively influential in America and is the core inspiration for Avatar: The Last Airbender, and the absurdly raunchy Panty & Stocking with Garterbelt, which is intentionally styled to look like an Adult Swim or MTV cartoon from the early 2000's. In all of its projects, Gainax is known for mind-blowing experimental action and style.

Collapse

Gainax controversially mismanaged their money and was even convicted of tax evasion following Evangelion's success. Following this and many other incidents such as lawsuits, arrests, and an outright refusal to produce any anime projects as opposed to sitting on piles of cash, Gainax went bankrupt and dissolved in 2024. However, the core teams that made up Gainax in its glory days had already split off into two new, successful studios: Anno and Evangelion became Studio Khara, and the rest split off after making Gurren Lagann to form Studio Trigger, the only major anime studio that still produces mostly original projects today (and in my opinion, one of the most stylish studios out there. Too bad they haven't made the cut for this website just yet.)
