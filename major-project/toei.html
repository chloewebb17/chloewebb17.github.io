Toei Animation

History

Toei animation is the oldest studio on this list by a significant margin. Toei was first founded in 1948 under the name Japan Animated Films. Studio Ghibli legends Hayao Miyazaki and the late Isao Takahata met and got their start when they worked at Toei. For a long time, Toei was pretty much the only anime studio, until Mushi Production challenged it with its 1960's hits. During this time, Japan was recovering from the second World War and nothing of note would be produced for about a decade. In the late 50's, Toei began producing many shows a year and the anime industry would slowly be born. At the time, anime and manga were heavily influenced by Western cartoons, which were thriving. In its life, Toei has producd nearly 500 projects, many of which have dozens if not hundreds of episodes. Between the 70's-90's, a good portion of popular anime projects were produced by Toei- and I'm not talking about specific gems or anything- Toei had an absolute stranglehold on TV and Pop Culture. These days, it's highly unlikely for Toei to pick up something new, especially when looking at what's popular right now. Toei is very happy with the IPs it has and continues to expand upon that work instead.

Specialty

Toei is responsible for some of the longest running anime IPs in the world. Saint Seita, Sailor Moon, Pretty Cure, Dragon Ball, Digimon, and of course One Piece have something in common. They're not just one or even a few seasons. Toei has systematically created several Simpson's length epics that have represented a constant in children's television since the late 80's. For many shows, instead of using a seasonal model like every other anime studio, Toei produces the show weekly, and has maintained a consistent airing schedule for decades. And if Toei doesn't follow the weekly model, the project may have dozens of seasons or spinoffs or more. Despite not having any new IPs, Toei is producing 9 series concurrently and has no plans on stopping. What makes this viable from an artistic standpoint is that Toei almost exclusively produces shows for children. Think of their work as saturday morning cartoons, a consistent backbone where other studios produce artistic, but less financially viable shows for teens and adults. Toei's shows are a staple in not only Japan but the rest of the world as well, through partnerships and licensing with foreign companies. The cultural staying power of Toei's shows is not to be underestimated. Pretty Cure, which has 23 series in the franchise, carries the entire magical girl genre on its back. One Piece has thousands of episodes and is the best selling manga series in the world, and is very very close to outselling Superman..

Dragon Ball

Mamoru Oshii is a highly acclaimed Japanese filmmaker. He worked on Kerberos Panzer Cop and later its film adaptation Jin-Roh: the Wolf Brigade with Production I.G. He also directed Angel's Egg and most famously, Ghost in the Shell. Some who worked on Angel's Egg went on to found Studio Ghibli. What's most notable about Ghost in the Shell is that it was a massive hit, even in America, when anime was relatively unknown and unpopular aside from the occasional Ghibli film. Ghost in the Shell was so popular overseas that it attracted the attention of the Wachowskis, who credit it as a primary inspiration for The Matrix. Though Ghost in the Shell was based on a manga of the same name, the film went on to recieve multiple original sequels, TV series, web series, and a very bad live-action film starring Scarlett Johansson. Today, Ghost in the Shell is recieving a new anime series adapatation by Science SARU that is independant of the Production I.G franchise and instead takes its inspiration from the manga, but it remains clear that this new adaptation is only possible because of the raging success of Mamoru Oshii's film.
