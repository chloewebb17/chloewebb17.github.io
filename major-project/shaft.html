Shaft

History

Shaft was founded by ex-Mushi Production member Hiroshi Wakao as a limited subcontractor for larger studios, which at the time was cel painting. Over the next few decades, the company slowly advanced the breadth of its work until through more complex collaborations until it was ready to create its own original projects. At the time, they had recently brought on Akiyuki Shinbo, who had a unique directorial style and was tasked with creating an identifiable style for the studio with its first full projects. A few years later, they produced their first two smash-hits: Bakemonogatari and Puella Magi Madoka Magica.

Specialty

Toei is responsible for some of the longest running anime IPs in the world. Saint Seita, Sailor Moon, Pretty Cure, Dragon Ball, Digimon, and of course One Piece have something in common. They're not just one or even a few seasons. Toei has systematically created several Simpson's length epics that have represented a constant in children's television since the late 80's. For many shows, instead of using a seasonal model like every other anime studio, Toei produces the show weekly, and has maintained a consistent airing schedule for decades. And if Toei doesn't follow the weekly model, the project may have dozens of seasons or spinoffs or more. Despite not having any new IPs, Toei is producing 9 series concurrently and has no plans on stopping. What makes this viable from an artistic standpoint is that Toei almost exclusively produces shows for children. Think of their work as saturday morning cartoons, a consistent backbone where other studios produce artistic, but less financially viable shows for teens and adults. Toei's shows are a staple in not only Japan but the rest of the world as well, through partnerships and licensing with foreign companies. The cultural staying power of Toei's shows is not to be underestimated. Pretty Cure, which has 23 series in the franchise, carries the entire magical girl genre on its back. One Piece has thousands of episodes and is the best selling manga series in the world, and is very, very close to outselling Superman.

Staff Exodus

Dragon Ball is a manga retelling of Journey to the West by artist Akira Toriyama, highly inspired by martial arts films and Chinese art, the latter of which was popular in Japan due to an improved relationship between the two countries at the time. Manga with Chinese influence popped up everywhere, like Dragon Ball, Dorohedoro, Ranma 1/2, Fullmetal Alchemist, and Fruits Basket. Fraught relations between these two countries have rendered this fad obselete, though. Because of Toei's licensing deals across the world, Dragon Ball ended up on completely free-to-air broadcast channel, which meant millions could watch it without paying for cable. The comfort of a consistently airing, good quality, free show that was highly unique to Latin America made Dragon Ball an instant classic all across the region. When Akira Toriyama died in 2024, the world mourned, especially Latin Americans who had been so impacted by his work.
